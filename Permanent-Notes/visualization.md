# Aphantasia

= 心盲症/想像障礙

[Aphantasia](https://www.google.com/search?q=Aphantasia) is the inability to voluntarily create mental images in one’s mind. People with aphantasia do not “see” anything when asked to picture an apple, a sunset, or a loved one’s face.

If you have aphantasia, traditional visualization techniques (e.g., “picture yourself succeeding”) may not work. Alternative approaches include verbal [self-talk](be-careful-how-you-are-talking-to-yourself.md), writing down scenarios, or drawing them physically.

> **Example:** A software engineer with aphantasia might struggle with “imagine the user interface” but excel at “describe the data flow step by step.” Their mental representations are abstract and verbal rather than visual.

# Mental Representations

= 心智表徵

Your skill in any area is a function of the quantity and quality of **Mental Representations** you have for that activity.

They are the internal models that allow you to recognize patterns, anticipate outcomes, and respond effectively.

As you [practice deliberately](deliberate-practice.md), your mental representations become more nuanced, detailed, and interconnected. A chess grandmaster doesn’t just see individual pieces — they see clusters of patterns, strategic themes, and future possibilities.

Mental representations built in one domain can sometimes transfer to related domains. For example, a musician who visualizes a performance uses the same mental representation skills that can help with public speaking or athletic performance.

# Negative Visualization

= Mental Contrasting = Fantasy Realization Theory

* Visualizing failure and the negative consequences can be more effective than focusing on positive outcomes alone.
* Visualize the negativities (e.g., disappointment, regret, pity, etc.) if you fail to achieve your goal, rather than simply visualize the goal you want to achieve — Rather than focus on the actions required for success. Consider what would lead to failure and avoid it.
* Seems to be particularly effective when it is combined with “[Implementation Intentions](implementation-intentions.md)” — the “if-then” plans that specify exactly _when_ and _where_ you will take action.
* Harnessing the power of _anticipated regret_, which involves envisioning the potential disappointment you’ll feel in the future if you don’t take certain actions today, can guide you to [make decisions](decision-making.md) that your future self will truly appreciate.

# The WOOP Framework

> developed by [Gabriele Oettingen](https://www.google.com/search?q=Gabriele+Oettingen)

1. **Wish:** Consider your wish — something challenging but achievable.
2. **Outcome:** Imagine the best possible outcome — let yourself indulge in the positive fantasy.
3. **Obstacle:** Identify the most critical inner obstacle that stands in the way — this is the step that distinguishes WOOP from simple positive thinking.
4. **Plan:** Make an if-then plan: “If [obstacle occurs], then I will [take this action].”

---

Engaging first in the “positive fantasy (daydreams)” followed by an acknowledgement of the “negative reality” — this contrast creates mental tension that [energizes action](cultivate-a-strong-bias-towards-action.md).

Positive visualization alone can trick your brain into feeling like you’ve already achieved the goal. Negative visualization keeps the gap between current reality and desired outcome salient.

**Use positive visualization for skill execution and goal clarity; use negative visualization for motivation and risk mitigation.**

Examples

* Visualize the regret of skipping a workout rather than just the satisfaction of being fit.
* Before a procrastination-prone task, vividly imagine how you’ll feel if you don’t start — the anxiety, the rush, the subpar result.
* Picture the regret of an impulsive purchase versus the peace of mind from saving.
* Imagine the emotional cost of not having a difficult conversation versus the relief after having it.
