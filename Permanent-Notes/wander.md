> “Not all those who wander are lost.” — J.R.R. Tolkien

---

Wandering is a deliberate permission to be aimless — to follow curiosity instead of a plan, to notice detours, and to prioritize discovery over [productivity](productivity.md).

---

# How to practice wandering

* Walk without a destination: take a different street or turn at the next corner.
* Read sideways: follow citations, footnotes, and tangents instead of only main body.
* Micro-wanders: give yourself 20–60 minutes a day to explore a single odd curiosity with no output requirement.
* Create friction for plans: remove your navigation app for a short walk, or bring only one notecard and a pen.
* Keep a “wander log”: capture one surprising observation or idea after each session.

# Wandering as a creative methodology

Wandering isn’t just a leisure activity — it’s a structured way to surface novelty. The key insight is that **you cannot discover what you aren’t looking for by looking for it.** You have to stop looking and let the discovery come to you. [^1]

Penicillin, microwaves, and Post-it notes were all discovered by someone who noticed something strange while pursuing something else.

# How to know you’re wandering correctly

* You lose track of time.
* You stumble onto something you weren’t looking for.
* You feel a small spark of curiosity, then follow it without judging it.
* Afterward, you can point to one thing — a thought, an image, a connection, an insight, a question, a feeling — that wasn’t there before.
* You return to your planned work with slightly fresher eyes.
* _You start to think you are wasting your time._ The discomfort of doing something “unproductive” is the right signal.

# Famous wanderers

* **Steve Jobs** took long walks — sometimes barefoot — as a deliberate thinking practice. He held walking meetings and credited meandering walks with some of his best ideas.
* **Albert Einstein** famously said, “I take my most important walks alone.” His thought experiments — chasing a beam of light, riding alongside a falling elevator — were acts of intellectual wandering that reshaped physics.
* **Virginia Woolf** was a devoted flâneuse [^2]. Her essay “Street Haunting” frames a walk to buy a pencil as an excuse to wander London and let the city feed the imagination.
* **Friedrich Nietzsche** declared that “only thoughts reached by walking have value,” and his most influential works were composed during his daily vigorous hikes.
